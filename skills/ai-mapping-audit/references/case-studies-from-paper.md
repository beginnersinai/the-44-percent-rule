# The 4 Case Studies from the Research Paper

These are the actual case studies shown to the treatment group in Kim, Kim & Koning (2026). These four examples caused firms to discover 44% more AI use cases. When showing these to users, present them in plain English — no jargon.

---

## Case Study 1: Gamma — Rethink the Whole Process, Not Just One Step

**What they do:** AI-native presentations company ($50M+ annual revenue, ~50 employees)

**How product development worked BEFORE AI:**
Nine people in a chain, each doing one step in a fixed quarterly cycle:
1. Product manager collects user feedback
2. Product manager prioritizes what to build
3. Product manager writes a detailed spec
4. Designer designs the feature
5. Engineer builds it
6. QA tester tests it
7. Data scientist runs an A/B test
8. Engineer deploys it
9. Support team handles any issues

**How it works NOW with AI:**
Five steps, one person can run the whole thing continuously:
1. AI detects usage patterns automatically
2. Product manager creates AI evaluation criteria
3. AI generates multiple product variants
4. Variants get A/B tested and evaluated
5. Best version gets deployed

**What changed:** A single product manager now continuously ships features that used to require an entire team working in quarterly cycles. The team didn't just add AI to one step — they redesigned the entire process around what AI can do.

**The lesson for your business:** Don't ask "where can I add AI to my existing process?" Ask "if AI could do most of the work, what would the process look like from scratch?"

## Case Study 2: RyzLabs — Build Three Versions Instead of Betting on One

**The old way of building a product:**
Sequential and expensive — commit to one approach and hope it works:
1. Come up with an idea
2. Choose a technology stack
3. Hire developers
4. Build one version (often takes months)
5. Test it with customers
6. If it doesn't work, pivot or start over

**The new way with AI:**
Parallel and nearly free:
1. Come up with an idea
2. Write a product requirements document with AI
3. Build the same idea simultaneously in three different AI coding tools (like Bolt, Lovable, and Replit)
4. Test the best version with a real customer
5. Build new features live on the call based on their feedback
6. Test again immediately

**What changed:** When building costs almost nothing, you can try everything at once instead of making one big bet. The bottleneck is no longer "can we build it?" — it's "how fast can we get customer feedback?"

**The lesson for your business:** What are you currently spending weeks building that you could prototype in an afternoon with AI? What would you try if building it was free?

## Case Study 3: FazeShift — Stop Being the Human Glue Between Your Software

**What they do:** AI accounts receivable startup

**How the work happened BEFORE:**
Eight steps alternating between software and humans manually bridging between them:
1. Data sits in a spreadsheet
2. A clerk pulls the data out
3. Clerk enters it into QuickBooks
4. Another clerk checks the bank portal
5. Clerk manually matches payments to invoices
6. Clerk opens Gmail to send follow-up emails
7. Clerk updates the spreadsheet
8. Repeat

**How it works NOW:**
End-to-end automation — humans only handle exceptions:
1. AI pulls data from the spreadsheet and reconciles it
2. QuickBooks gets updated automatically
3. AI matches payments to invoices
4. AI sends follow-up emails from templates
5. A human specialist only steps in for unusual cases that need judgment

**What changed:** The clerks weren't doing complex work — they were moving information between systems that don't talk to each other. AI replaced that "glue work" entirely. The remaining human role shifted from data entry to expert judgment on edge cases.

**The lesson for your business:** Think about your daily workflow. How much time do you spend copying information from one tool to another? Moving data between spreadsheets, emails, CRMs, and documents? That's glue work, and AI can do almost all of it.

## Case Study 4: Ranger — Sell First, Build AI from What You Learn

**The traditional startup path (raise money first):**
All the money goes out before any comes in:
1. Raise money from investors
2. Hire a team
3. Build the product
4. Try to sell it to customers
5. Run the service
6. Scale revenue (hopefully)

**The AI-enabled path (sell first):**
Revenue comes before investment:
1. Sell the service yourself from day one (founder does the work manually)
2. Learn which steps are routine and repetitive
3. Build AI to automate those routine steps
4. Hire a small team to work alongside the AI
5. Scale revenue and improve margins
6. Raise money last — if you even need it

**What changed:** "Raise money" moved from the first step to the last. AI makes it possible to deliver a service at near-zero cost while you learn exactly what to automate. You don't need to guess what customers want — you've been doing the work yourself.

**The lesson for your business:** What services could you offer today, do manually at first, and then build AI to handle the routine parts? You don't need funding or a team to start — you need customers and the willingness to learn what's repetitive.

## The Pattern Across All Four

Every case study shows the same thing:

1. **The old process was sequential** — one step at a time, one person per step
2. **Adding AI to one step didn't transform anything** — it just made one step slightly faster
3. **Redesigning the whole process around AI created massive gains** — fewer steps, fewer people needed, faster cycles
4. **The human role shifted** — from doing routine work to making judgment calls and setting direction

This is the O-Ring principle from the paper: when a process has many linked steps, improving just one step doesn't help much. The chain is only as fast as its slowest link. The real gains come from rethinking the entire chain.
